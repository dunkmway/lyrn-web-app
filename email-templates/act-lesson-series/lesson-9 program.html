English - Sentence Composition

Sentence Composition is the most important topic on the English section of the ACT. As such, you can expect to see more than 20 of these questions on every test. You can recognize these questions either because they involve punctuation or they are changing the clause structure of the sentence.

An independent clause (IC) contains a subject, verb, and a complete sentence; a dependent clause (DC) will contain a subject and a verb, but not a complete thought; extra information (EI) is any phrase (or clause) that is not required to maintain the meaning of the sentence. With these definitions, the following table will show the possible sentence constructs that you will see on the ACT.

IC. DC, IC. IC. IC. IC: EI. I (EI) C.
IC; IC. IC—EI. I—EI—C
IC, FANBOYS IC. IC, EI. I, EI, C.

The key to solving these questions is to insert each answer choice (one by one), break down the sentence, partitioned by punctuation, and identify the sentence that follows one of the rules shown in the table.

For example,

Her interest in this theme grew directly from her once difficult relationship with her mother and Tan describes her teen-aged self as "every mother's nightmare."

A. NO CHANGE

B. mother: Tan

C. mother, and she also

D. mother, Tan

We know this is a sentence composition problem because every answer choice involves punctuation (with one option removing the punctuation). In option A, there is no punctuation, thus we will look at the entire sentence as a whole. In this case, we have two subjects (her and Tan) and thus should have two separate sentences making option A an invalid sentence. Option B uses a colon. If we read everything before the colon, we get an IC which is what we want. Reading everything after the colon is another IC. However, using the table above, 'IC: IC' is not an a valid sentence composition; thus, option B is also wrong. For Option C, we have a comma + FANBOYS (and). Both the clause before and after the comma and FANBOYS are ICs. Looking at the table above, 'IC, FANBOYS IC' is a valid sentence: giving us C as the correct answer.
