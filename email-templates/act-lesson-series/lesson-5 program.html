English - Simplicity

Simplicity is the second most important topic on the English section of the ACT. As such, you can expect to see 7 - 8 of these questions on every test. You can recognize these questions either because the answers will all say pretty much the same thing except that some of the choices include unnecessary information and are generally very verbose or the answer choices sound very unusual either being out of place in the context of the passage or contain redundant information.

The approach here is to eliminate any answer that:

  • repeats information previously stated in the passage (usually the same paragraph)
  • repeats information within the answer choice
  • adds fluff (wordier answer choices)
  • uses casual, less formal language

For example,

Two years later, she won the vote and was elected Campus Queen.

A. NO CHANGE

B. was elected and voted in as

C. won an election in which the students voted for

D. was elected.

We know that this is a simplicity question because the four answer choices state basically the same thing. Additionally, there is verbose language and needless repetition. In A, to win a vote has the same meaning as being elected, thus, it has redundancy within the answer choice. Redundancy is always bad and can be eliminated. Just like A, B and C both mention being voted for and being elected within their answer choices; therefore, they can both be removed. That leaves us with D as the correct answer since it is the simplest way to state the information without any repetition.
